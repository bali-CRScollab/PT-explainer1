The worked example
This tool models Verra's AFOLU (Agriculture, Forestry & Other Land Use) buffer pool — the largest voluntary carbon registry buffer in existence. Verra currently holds over 77 million tonnes in its buffer reserve, drawn from a land-based portfolio estimated at roughly 500 million tonnes, worth roughly $10bn. That buffer represents capital ($1.5bn) locked away indefinitely, with no climate return and no option to redeploy it into additional removal capacity, even if no reversals occur. This is capital the Permanence Trust plans to put to work.

Tiered coverage — what the Gold/Silver/Bronze labels mean
GOLD — reserve fully covers the inflation-adjusted reversal target (≥100%). Every tonne of potential reversal is backed 1:1. SILVER — reserve covers ≥25% of liability, meaningfully above typical 15–20% buffer pool coverage but below full 1:1 backing. BRONZE — reserve exceeds the ~3% global expected worst annual loss rate but is below buffer-pool-equivalent coverage. A floor, not a ceiling. Below 3% is flagged as materially insufficient.
Coverage assumption — two very different targets
Capital held in this Permanence Trust Model is explicitly designed to grow toward 100% replacement of credits. Meanwhile buffer pools withhold on average only 10–20% of credits and can cover only that withheld fraction — the remaining 80–90% of sold credits have no financial backstop if they reverse.
The models are not symmetric: one is a partial reserve, the other targets full liability coverage.
What "coverage" means here — modelling simplification
Throughout the tool, "coverage" means: does the reserve hold enough capital to buy back the full liability in same-pathway ARR at future appreciation-adjusted prices? An ARR tonne reversed, replaced with an ARR tonne. This is the conservative floor.

In practice, any reversal can be covered by any credible pathway — ARR, biochar, ERW, DACCS — at prevailing cost, availability, and durability. A durable replacement insures against future reversals too. There's also a strategic choice: over-retire liability with durable removals (the Section 04 horizontal-stacking view), or waterfall — keep the reserve liquid and draw only as reversals materialise (closer to traditional insurance). The tool's coverage metric is a floor, not a ceiling — under optimal pathway mix, real-world replacement capacity would be higher.

Pathway durability — why DACCS is the default proxy
The tool uses DACCS as the anchor for durable engineered removals because it is effectively geologically permanent (~1,000+ years, >99% retention), making the "100% durable hedge" assumption cleanest. Biochar is materially cheaper but reflects Woolf-decay losses over 100 years, with durability typically estimated at 75–90% (recent Inertinite Benchmark work is tightening this). Enhanced rock weathering sits between, with modest far-field loss factors reducing realised sequestration. In a full model, each pathway would carry its own durability discount — a biochar tonne doesn't retire a full tonne of ARR liability the way a DACCS tonne does. The preset above adjusts prices, but durability remains an open question worth probing separately.
