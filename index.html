The worked example
This tool models Verra's AFOLU (Agriculture, Forestry & Other Land Use) buffer pool — the largest voluntary carbon registry buffer in existence. Verra currently holds over 77 million tonnes in its buffer reserve, drawn from a land-based portfolio estimated at roughly 500 million tonnes, worth roughly $10bn. That buffer represents capital ($1.5bn) locked away indefinitely, with no climate return and no option to redeploy it into additional removal capacity, even if no reversals occur. This is capital the Permanence Trust plans to put to work.

Coverage assumption — two very different targets
Capital held in this Permanence Trust Model is explicitly designed to grow toward 100% replacement of credits. Meanwhile buffer pools withhold on average only 10–20% of credits and can cover only that withheld fraction — the remaining 80–90% of sold credits have no financial backstop if they reverse.
The models are not symmetric: one is a partial reserve, the other targets full liability coverage.

So why does this work?

i.
Time is on our side.
Emissions reduction is the urgent task. That window is long enough for capital to compound.
ii.
Supply across all pathways.
Not just one. Planetary boundaries mean we can't rely on nature alone.
iii.
Empirical pricing.
Quality rewarded, risk visible — instead of arbitrary withholding.
It was never nature versus technology. It just needed a market designed to treat permanence as an obligation, not an afterthought.
